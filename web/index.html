01

The gap

Nikud describes formal grammar. Spoken Hebrew diverges.

Standard G2P predicts vowel diacritics and prescriptive rules. Everyday speech shifts vowels, simplifies conjunctions, and pronounces loanwords by ear.

ReNikud learns from thousands of hours of real speech with character-level alignment — avoiding seq2seq hallucinations on limited text data.

Abstract

Grapheme-to-phoneme conversion for Modern Hebrew is needed for text-to-speech, but the abjad writing system leaves vowels unwritten. Standard approaches predict nikud to produce IPA — yet vocalization data is scarce, stress is underspecified, and rules reflect formal grammar rather than everyday speech.

ReNikud uses weak audio supervision via an ASR pseudo-labeling pipeline on thousands of hours of unlabeled Hebrew audio, plus a pseudo-vocalization architecture that predicts IPA at each character position. On Hebrew G2P benchmarks and targeted test suites for spoken Hebrew, ReNikud surpasses prior state-of-the-art.
